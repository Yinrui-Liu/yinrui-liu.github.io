I am an experimental particle physicist, with a focus on the upcoming Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment (DUNE) during my PhD. We aim to use neutrinos to probe the mystery of matter-antimatter asymmetry. My work spans many aspects of DUNE and beyond, with a growing interest in AI/ML applications in particle physics. Below are highlights of some of my research work during my PhD.

Transformer Model for Particle Propagation within the Nucleus

When we detect a neutrino through its interaction with a nucleus, it creates particles that bounce around inside the nucleus before escaping (as shown in the animation below), making it difficult to reconstruct what actually happened. This effect, called "final-state interactions" (FSI), has been a focus of neutrino interaction modeling. Working with Kazuhiro Terao and Patrick Tsang at SLAC, we developed a transformer model‚Äîsimilar to those used in language processing‚Äîthat learns to predict particle propagation within the nucleus. Just as language models treat words as tokens, our model treats different particle types as tokens, using their positions and four-momenta as feature variables. Our current results have achieved a 97% accuracy in predicting particle types, with ongoing work to expand the training dataset and apply this new method to generating neutrino events. [GitHub]

Generative Models for Fast Simulation of LArTPC Events

As particle physics experiments become more sophisticated, traditional computer simulations may become a bottleneck. For example, simulating a single neutrino event for DUNE can take longer than actually recording the event in its near detector. Machine learning offers a promising solution by learning to generate realistic particle interactions from existing simulation data. I'm developing generative models that treat each particle event as a cloud of energy deposits, training them to create new events that look and behave like real physics. The figures below compare original simulation events by Geant4 (top row) with AI-generated events (bottom row) for electron showers and proton tracks. While the AI successfully replicates some visual patterns, ongoing studies ensure the generated events maintain the correct physics.

Hadron-Argon Cross-Section Measurements at ProtoDUNE (PhD Thesis)

For my PhD thesis, I led the first-ever measurements of how positive pions and protons interact with argon nuclei at the energies relevant to DUNE. These "cross-section" measurements tell us how often these particles collide with argon atoms, which is crucial for modeling neutrino interactions in liquid argon experiments like DUNE. I developed new statistical methods and analysis framework to extract these measurements from ProtoDUNE data [Instruments, GitHub]. The results are currently under DUNE internal review, with a publication to be submitted to Physical Review Letters. The figures below show our main results, with measured cross sections overlaid on theoretical predictions. No significant tension is observed given the current experimental uncertainties.

Final-State Interaction (FSI) Modeling in Neutrino Generators

Building on my cross-section measurements, I collaborated with Stephen Dolan and Laura Munteanu at CERN to study how different interaction models affect neutrino reconstruction. When neutrinos interact with atomic nuclei, the resulting particles can scatter multiple times before escaping‚Äîa process called "final-state interactions" (FSI). This is why the cross-section measurements are crucial, as they provide essential data to inform these FSI models in neutrino generators. We conducted several studies on FSI modeling, with a theory paper to be released soon, which demonstrates how uncertainties in these models can mask genuine neutrino oscillation signals. Using DUNE as a case study, the figures below show how different modeling choices can obscure the true oscillation effects we want to measure, particularly visible from the bottom ratio plots.

Detector Modules Production and Installation for DUNE

DUNE's design centers on liquid argon time projection chamber (LArTPC) technology, with a prevailing design that uses multiple layers of sensing wires as readout devices. These wire planes, called anode plane assemblies (APAs), are the major detector components of DUNE's first far detector (FD1). This page provides a detailed description of how LArTPC technology works with wire plane readout. The University of Chicago operates an APA factory to produce detector modules for DUNE FD1, where I work on preparing the bare frames before they move to a robotic "winder" machine. The figures below show an APA frame on the winder and a video of the winder winding the sensing wires. In addition to the production work, I also spent time at CERN in 2022 for ProtoDUNE installation and in 2024 as a data shifter during summer operations.

Observation of the First Doubly-Charged Tetraquark Particle

Normal hadronic particles are made of either two or three quarks, called mesons and baryons. Only in this century have we discovered particles made of four and five quarks, called tetraquarks and pentaquarks‚Äîcollectively known as "exotic hadrons." In my early PhD, I continued my undergraduate research on hadron spectroscopy using LHCb data. I made major contributions to a generic framework for partial wave analysis based on TensorFlow (called TF-PWA), and applied it to analyze a pair of related $B$ meson decays. I led the analysis that resulted in the observation of the first doubly-charged tetraquark $T^a_{c\bar{s}0}(2900)^{++}$ and its neutral partner $T^a_{c\bar{s}0}(2900)^{0}$, with publications in PRL and PRD. The figures below show the tetraquark signal in the mass spectrum and a CERN artist's impression of its structure.
